# PROVEN Brand Voice

**Brand philosophy:** Recognition over aspiration. Make people feel deeply understood, not algorithmically categorized.

## The Five Qualities

Every piece of PROVEN copy must express all five of these qualities simultaneously. They are not optional or situational. They are the voice.

### 1. Simplicity and Clarity

Write in clear, declarative sentences. Strip away jargon and technical complexity, making even sophisticated science feel approachable. The Skin Genome Project analyzes 28 million data points, but the copy never feels like a research paper. It feels like someone who understands you, speaking plainly.

- Say "your skin gets exactly what it needs," not "algorithmically optimized dermatological formulation"
- If a word can be simpler, make it simpler
- If a sentence can be shorter, make it shorter
- The reader should never have to re-read a line

> "One formula. Yours."
> "Skincare that knows your skin."

### 2. Confidence Without Arrogance

Let the evidence make the case. PROVEN's confidence comes from what's verifiable: the data, the patents, the outcomes. State facts plainly and let them do the work. Don't argue, don't hedge, don't over-explain. The science is real. Present it and move on.

- No hedging: eliminate "we believe," "we think," "arguably," "perhaps," "might"
- No defensive stacking: never "the most incredible, amazing, revolutionary..."
- No unbacked superlatives: if you can't point to a number or a source, rephrase
- No justifying: if the evidence is clear, it needs a period, not a paragraph
- Confidence is quiet. It doesn't exclaim. Periods over exclamation marks, always.

> "47 skin factors. 28 million data points. One formula, designed for you."
> "93% of customers reported improvement within 30 days."

Not: "We think our personalization technology is probably the most advanced currently available on the market."
Not: "The most personalized skincare on earth." (Unless immediately backed by specific evidence.)

### 3. Human Warmth Underneath Precision

The writing is minimal and controlled, but never cold. Connect data to experiences and emotions: how the skin *feels*, not just what the algorithm did. The science is the credibility. The feeling is the story.

- Every data point or feature must bridge to a human feeling
- Warmth lives in directness, not decoration. No performed enthusiasm, no empty flattery.
- Specificity is the mechanism for warmth. "Your water hardness" is warmer than "we care about your skin" because it proves attention. The more specific the detail, the warmer the copy feels.
- No fear-based language: lead with the solution and the feeling, not the anxiety
- The customer should feel recognized, not sold to

> "47 skin factors analyzed to build a routine that finally feels like yours."
> "Your skin changes with stress, with seasons, with time. Your skincare adapts with it."

Not: "Fight the signs of aging with our revolutionary anti-wrinkle combat system!"
Not: "We care deeply about your skin's health." (Vague warmth. Says nothing specific.)

### 4. Conversational but Elevated

Avoid corporate-speak but also avoid being too casual or trendy. The register sits in a sweet spot: like a very smart friend who happens to speak beautifully. The reader feels respected, not talked down to. The language is premium without being pretentious, accessible without being dumbed down.

- No corporate-speak: eliminate "leverage," "synergy," "ecosystem," "solution," "utilize"
- No generic beauty-speak: eliminate "unlock your glow," "reveal your radiance," "miracle formula"
- No try-hard casual: no slang, no memes, no forced relatability
- Read it aloud. It should sound like confident, elegant speech, not a press release and not a text message.

> "Every skin is different. Not in the vague way beauty brands say it, but in the measurable, provable way."
> "You've always known your skin was different. Now your skincare knows it too."

### 5. Rhythm and Craft

Every word must earn its place. The copy should feel as designed as the product, where nothing could be added and nothing could be removed. But inevitable does not mean mechanical. The goal is writing that sounds like a thoughtful person who chose every word carefully, not a copywriter performing minimalism.

- PROVEN needs to work at every length. A tagline that lands in four words. A product page that sustains across a full paragraph. The voice must carry both.
- Fragments are powerful when they're earned. "One formula. Yours." works because it's compressed to its purest form. But when every sentence in a paragraph is a fragment, the power disappears and the writing becomes unreadable. Fragments punch hardest when surrounded by sentences that flow.
- Vary sentence length naturally. A short sentence hits harder after a longer one.
- "Natural" does not mean "loose." Every sentence should still be as tight as it can be while sounding human. If a word doesn't change the meaning, cut it. If a clause doesn't advance the idea, remove it. Economy and readability are not opposites.
- End sentences on strong words when you can, but not at the expense of sounding natural.
- Parallel structure works for emphasis, but don't force every paragraph into the same repeating pattern.
- Read it aloud. If it sounds like someone actually talking with precision, you're close. If it sounds like a manifesto, a poetry exercise, or a rambling email, rewrite.

> **Compressed (tagline, social, punchline):**
> "One formula. Yours."
> "Personalized down to the molecule."
>
> **Flowing (product page, email, longer copy):**
> "Your skin responds to humidity, stress, and the slow shift between seasons. We built the science to understand all of it, so your skincare finally can too."
>
> Notice the difference. Both are PROVEN. The compressed version lands a single idea with maximum impact. The flowing version sustains an idea across two sentences that read like a person talking. Match the mode to the format.

## Avoid (Always)

- Exclamation marks (confidence doesn't need them)
- Passive voice in hero copy
- Rhetorical questions in headlines
- Superlative stacking
- Unbacked superlative claims without specific evidence alongside them
- The word "custom" — always use "personalized" (custom = made-to-order; personalized = the system knows you)
- Fear-based framing: "fight aging," "combat wrinkles," "battle breakouts"
- Qualifying language: "we believe," "we think," "arguably"
- Filler words: "very," "really," "actually," "just," "quite," "truly"
- Making personalization sound creepy or surveillance-like
- Generic copy that could appear on any competitor's site

## Writing Naturally (Anti-AI Guard)

PROVEN copy should read like it was written by a person with taste, not generated by a machine trained on brand decks. The problem is never fragments, punchlines, or compressed copy in themselves. The problem is using them as the default mode for everything, which makes the writing unreadable and obviously AI-generated. Watch for these patterns and eliminate them:

- **Fragments everywhere.** "Personalized formulas. Clean ingredients. Real results." as a tagline is fine. As the construction for every sentence in a product page or email, it's a dead giveaway of AI. Match the construction to the format.
- **Em dash overuse.** One em dash per paragraph is plenty. If you're using them in every sentence, rewrite. Commas, colons, and periods are usually better.
- **Stacked parallel structure.** "The way your skin... The way it... The way it..." repeated three or more times sounds algorithmic. Vary the construction.
- **Patterned rhythm.** If every paragraph follows the same short-short-short-long structure, the reader feels the machinery. Mix it up.

But "natural" is not an excuse to get loose. PROVEN copy avoids AI patterns AND avoids the opposite failure:

- **Chattiness.** If a sentence could be tighter without sounding robotic, tighten it. Never add words for warmth that don't carry meaning. Conversational does not mean verbose.
- **Explaining too much.** State, don't explain. If you've made the point, stop. The reader is smart.
- **Drifting into generic DTC warmth.** "We get it, skin is complicated" is the kind of filler that sounds human but says nothing. Every sentence should contain an idea the reader couldn't have predicted.

The standard: every word inevitable, every sentence readable. If it sounds like AI, rewrite it. If it sounds like someone rambling naturally, tighten it. The sweet spot is a person who speaks with precision.

## Voice Examples by Format

**Tagline (maximum compression):**
> One formula. Yours.

**Social (compressed, one idea):**
> 100,000+ unique formulations. Every one built for one person.

**Hero headline + subhead:**
> Skincare that knows your skin.
> PROVEN analyzes 47 factors, from your genetics to your water hardness to how your skin responds to stress, and builds a formula no one else will ever receive.

**Data-to-feeling bridge:**
> We analyze 47 skin factors and 28 million data points to build one formula that's entirely yours.

**Product feature:**
> Your Personalized Night Cream is formulated for your skin's specific needs and works while you sleep. By morning, your skin feels different, because it is.

**Email opener:**
> Your skin changed this winter. Your formula already knows.

**CTA:**
> Take the two-minute quiz and we'll build your personalized formula.

**Longer form (body paragraph):**
> Rosacea-prone skin reacts to things most products ignore: your water hardness, seasonal humidity shifts, stress-triggered inflammation. PROVEN reads those factors and formulates around your specific triggers, not a generic rosacea protocol. When your sensitivity shifts through the year, your formula adjusts with it.

**Post-purchase (warm, evidence-led, expectation-setting):**
> Your routine was built from the 47 factors you shared with us, cross-referenced against 28 million data points. Every ingredient and concentration was selected for your skin, your environment, and your life right now. Give it a few weeks to calibrate. Your skin has been adapting to generic products for a long time, and the right formula needs a moment to show you what it can do.

**Recognition moment (quiz results):**
> Here's what we found. Your skin profile is shaped by 47 factors, and your formula was built around every one of them. The ingredients, the concentrations, the way your products work together — all of it was designed for your skin specifically. As your life changes, so will your formula.

## Application by Context

**Product pages:** All five qualities at full strength. Lead with a clean headline. Follow with data-to-feeling bridges. Close on recognition.

**Taglines and headlines:** Maximum compression. One idea. Land on the strongest word. Fragments are expected here.

**Email campaigns:** Slightly warmer, slightly more conversational. Still confident, still economical. Open with what matters to the reader, not the brand. Flowing sentences are fine here, but every one should still earn its place.

**Social media:** Shortest form. One idea per post. Fragments work here because the format demands compression.

**Ads (Meta, etc.):** Hook with one compressed idea. Bridge to feeling. CTA.

**Quiz and onboarding:** Warm, curious, respectful. Frame questions as recognition: "Tell us what you already know about your skin." The customer is intelligent. Never make them feel like a data subject. Keep the elevated register even when the tone is warm.

**Quiz results / recognition moment:** This is the most emotionally important touchpoint. Lead with what we found, not how we found it. The customer should feel seen, not processed. Open with the finding, then name what was built and why.

**Post-purchase:** Evidence-led reassurance. Name what was built and from what. Set realistic expectations for the calibration period. Respect the customer's patience as intelligence, not ask for it as a favor.

**Results and testimonials:** Let specificity persuade. Real numbers, real timelines, real outcomes. No embellishment needed.
